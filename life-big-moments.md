### 1984 - Hola Mundo! - "I opened my eyes!"

### 1988 - 1989 - Goodbye Mummy
Thank you... for bringing me into this world — *Mummy WW*. I think it was 1989, but since then we've been walking different paths. Maybe it was drilled into me from a young age not to care about you, or to believe that you cared. That might be a little cynical of my dad, but there's some truth in it. I turned out fine, **mostly**, in case you're wondering.

To be honest, I don't really know you, and I don't know where you are now either. My memories of early childhood are a blur, and we didn't have many opportunities to meet after the divorce. I think the last time I saw you was around Primary 6. I wish you well, and that you're healthy and happy.

### 1990 - The Start of Formal Education
My primary school was Red Swastika School, in Bedok. The most striking memory I have is of a classmate called Melvin. He was a skinny boy who was often bullied because of his clumsy, good-natured ways. It wasn't anything nasty — just the usual laughing at him and making tasteless jokes. I won't deny it; I was one of those who bullied him too, typical herd mentality, I guess. Yet one day I saw him with his grandmother. She was old and frail, but I could see how much she cared for her grandchild. That affected me.

"Why was I bullying her grandson? Wouldn't it hurt her to see him upset?" I took it upon myself to protect him and told the others off. That episode struck a chord with me. I've always lived with my grandparents, and they've been an instrumental part of my life.

I'm so thankful for my grandparents. I lived with them my whole life. They provided for me without complaint, cooked for me, didn't mind when I cancelled dinner to head out after school, protected me from my dad, and gave me a warm, loving environment to grow up in. I am who I am because of them.

In the early days, I recall mealtimes with my grandma chasing after me as I ran around playing in the garden. I was quite the brat. My grandfather used to pick me up from kindergarten on his bicycle, and I'd ride pillion home. My dad worked shifts, and with our schedules, there could be an entire week where I barely saw him. I was also a little afraid of him, and my grandmother was my partner in crime, helping me avoid getting scolded.

In primary school, my CCA choice was an unusual one: the harmonica. I even performed a couple of pieces on stage, usually hidden in the back row, just trying to be as quiet as possible. The funny thing is, I think I picked it because of a girl. No, not what you're thinking — she was a family friend, and my dad had an arrangement with her mother: he'd help send the two of us home when his schedule allowed, and on the other weeks her mother would help out. To make things more convenient, I picked the same CCA as her.

### 1996 - My First Computer
The impact of computers and technology on my life has been immense. Head over to [my gadgets page]({{ site.url }}{{ site.baseurl }}/gadgets/) for a full overview of my technolust.

My dad bought me this first computer as a reward after my PSLE, and I made good use of it. The brand was APEX. I still remember blowing the power supply barely a couple of months in, when I switched it from 220V to 110V. **Itchy fingers.**

Thankfully, I think the warranty covered the repair. Computers back then didn't have the internet, so the next best thing was games. I didn't know much, and the extent of my dabbling was changing the command prompt message and the colour of the font.

### 1997 - Drama in Secondary School
Once again, of all the CCAs available, I have no idea why I joined the English Drama Society in Dunman High, my home for the next four years. As it turned out, it was an interesting, if unexpected, decision. It gave me a lot of free time, though I'm not sure I grew much from the experience. I picked up some weird dance moves, and while I acted on stage, I don't think it helped much with my public speaking or presentation skills.

That said, compared to friends in more intensive CCAs, EDS gave me plenty of free time to dabble with my computers, especially on weekends. After Saturday CCA ended, I was quite the loner and would typically head home early. I looked forward to the weekends with my computers and games, which brings me to...

### 1999 - Unreal Tournament
Of all the games I've played — and there have been many — Unreal Tournament is undoubtedly the standout. It was the game I really dived deep into, and I even competed in multiplayer at a serious level.

Clan ngE — it was started by my secondary school friend, as we were the only two in school playing Unreal Tournament. It didn't grow much. At its peak, there were only four of us: my friend's brother and a rookie — an endearing 12-year-old named Psymasta. We were a small social group, and when I started playing more competitively, we disbanded and I joined one of the bigger clans in Singapore — X3M.

Did it affect my studies? Perhaps, but I think I grew from the competitive experience. In the first competition I entered, my team were quarter-finalists in the inaugural 3v3 tournament (SiliconHorizon 3v3 CTF) held in Singapore. I grew from that experience, and subsequently we came in second in my next two competitions — one at SAFRA, the other at Cineleisure. In 2001, with the start of JC, I started playing less, and shortly after, with a new Unreal Tournament version in 2002 and the pressures of JC, I "retired" from online gaming.

### 2001 - Junior College - VJC
Life in VJC was fun — perhaps too fun, as my studies took a backseat. Living about 1 km from school, I cycled or walked there and hung out with a great bunch of friends in class. This could have been the moment I dived deep into computers and programming — I was in the Robotics CCA — but I let the opportunity pass me by. Crazy moments included skipping class, producing a short film for a class project, receiving a scholarship award from Dr Tony Tan, and yet doing quite poorly for my 'A' levels. Finally, David Beckham visited our school, and I walked home with a prized soccer ball he kicked out of the field during a free-kick showcase.

### 2003 - Becoming an Air Engineering Officer
20 Jan 2003 was the day I enlisted into the army, Echo Company, as a *chao* recruit alongside plenty of others. The army experience was an eye-opener. But it did toughen me up. Along the way, with an uncertain future ahead, a career talk convinced me to pursue becoming an Air Engineering Officer. In hindsight, at 18 I wasn't sure what I wanted to do, and that was honestly a huge decision. Around the same time, I also started my tertiary education in 2003 at the National University of Singapore.

If you asked me why I signed on, I'd bluntly tell you the key factor was the money. The Air Force seemed like a decent place to work, the starting pay was attractive, and it was a good opportunity for me at that point in life. Still, convenience should never be the answer, and that lesson only became clearer to me as I got older.

### 2004 - My first trip overseas without my Dad
For the first time, I didn't travel with my dad. My first trip overseas was with N, as the two of us headed off to explore southeastern Australia — Sydney and Melbourne, to be exact. It was a growing-up trip. We did the touristy stuff and the seedy stuff, met up with friends studying abroad, bummed around in trashy hostels, rode a midnight train across town, emptied my pockets at a casino, and watched Greece unexpectedly lift Euro 2004 with a whole bunch of Australians.

### 2007 - Convocation to Cadet
I finally graduated after four years at university. Not too shabby. I made some close friends, snagged a girlfriend, and graduated with honours. The next phase of life soon started — a time of adjustment, back to the SAF and a life of regimentation.

### 2010 - She Said Yes!
Perhaps the grandest and most romantic gesture I'd ever planned: a surprise trip to Shanghai, where I plotted with her colleagues to surprise her at the World Expo Singapore Pavilion. It was a success! Hey, I even made the news.

### 2011 - Mr & Mrs
We walked down the aisle as husband and wife. It's been life-changing, but it was the best decision I've made in my whole life.

### 2012 - GadgetReactor
Bored in class, Zhaolin and I were trying to buy the Nook Color, a hackable e-book reader, and alongside it we launched GadgetReactor to talk about our experience. I've always been a big geek.

### 2012 - Leaving the Force
Leaving the military was a big decision. Where to next? Studies? Work? The five years there had undoubtedly been excellent, giving me precious opportunities to grow. But as I looked ahead, I felt it was time to hang up my rank and look towards a new life beyond the military. Time to remember this quote from Fight Club.

<blockquote>
<em>You're not your job. You're not how much money you have in the bank. You're not the car you drive. You're not the contents of your wallet. You're not your fucking khakis. You're the all-singing, all-dancing crap of the world.<small>Tyler Durden</small></em>
</blockquote>

### 2012 - Start Up Ecosystem
I found myself plonked into the world of SMEs and start-ups. Life in SPRING Singapore exposed me to a myriad of companies and the challenges they faced. I made the news again, but this time in a less positive light. The transition to SPRING did aid my journey forward. I wanted a bigger challenge — to be in the eye of the storm.

### 2014 - O2C
I was inspired by Jason and his vision for the company. We had dreams of making it big, but I also knew the journey was fraught with risks. We fought for two years, but industry demand wasn't as robust as we envisioned. It was an exciting journey — from networking with investors and closing deals to striking industry partnerships. It was also my first private-sector experience, where I got mired in a sea of operations and taught myself full-stack software engineering on a Django project. It was a highly enriching two years, and I think that experience landed me my tech-related job years later.
